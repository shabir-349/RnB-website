The Problem

Most medical students never publish.

Not because they lack curiosity or intelligence — they have both in abundance. But the path from "I want to do research" to an accepted manuscript is scattered across a dozen YouTube tutorials, three different textbooks, and a senior resident who might respond to your message in two weeks if you're lucky. The tools you need are either locked behind institutional licenses, buried in 400-page methodology guides, or simply absent.

Statistics software intimidates. Screening thousands of abstracts alone exhausts. Writing an IMRaD manuscript for the first time, without a mentor who has done it before, feels like assembling furniture with instructions in a language you half-learned. And for students in Pakistan, India, Egypt, or Nigeria — where mentors are overstretched and research culture is still growing — the barriers compound. By the time most students find the right guidance, their residency application deadline has passed.

Good research skills shouldn't be a privilege of geography or institutional prestige. Every student who can frame a clinical question deserves a clear, supported path to publication — with the right tools, the right feedback, and someone in their corner at every step.

"We built R&B so that doesn't have to happen anymore."
